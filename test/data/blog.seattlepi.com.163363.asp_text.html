Inner text: BARSTOW, Calif. -- Left Yreka, Calif., at 6:30 this morning and arrived here at the beautiful Barstow Super 8 at 10:30 this evening after 650 miles of driving and a few stops, the first in Sacramento to interview Spencer Hawes, the subject of Wednesday's column. Hawes was in good spirits and talkative as usual. I also spoke with Kings strength coach Daniel Shapiro, who is best friends with Andrew Moritz, a former Husky who is battling cancer and the subject of last Monday's column. The Kings are the worst team in the league and I had no idea who the coach was until someone pointed him out and told me he was Kenny Natt. Enjoyed talking to him about Hawes. Then spent maybe 20 minutes with Hawes, who was his quotable self. When I left, I reminisced about the great rivalry the Sonics had with the Kings in the 1990s. Hard to believe the Kings are the worst team in the NBA and the Sonics don't even exist anymore. As I drove out of the Arco Arena parking lot, I thought back to those days of covering the Sonics. I was a beat writer from 1990-96, and in that final year, or maybe it was the year before, I forget, and it's late ... anyway, this was the site of my worst-ever conflict with an athlete in my 26 years at the P-I. Figured now is as good a time as any to tell you what happened ... First round of the NBA playoffs, Sonics vs. Kings. Sonics win the first game at KeyArena but lose the second and head to Sacramento for Game 3. They were coming off two consecutive first-round eliminations against Denver and the Lakers, and everyone in town thought they were going to flame out again. At any rate, we found out that Gary Payton had to go to the hospital after Game 2 because he was so dehydrated he needed intravenous fluids. Thought that was a good story ... something along the lines of "Sonics lost, but their leader played so hard that he had to go to the hospital." So I pursued it before Game 3 in Sacramento. Went into the Sonics' locker room at Arco Arena before the game and asked Payton about it. He was tying his shoes and looked up at me and said: "What's that got to do with basketball?" I repeated the story, told him that it just showed a lot about him to play his heart out to the point that he had to go to the hospital, etc. "What's that got to do with basketball?" he repeated. I tried one more time to get him to play along and got one more "What's that got to do with basketball?" And then after years of growing tired of dealing with him, I got fed up and instead of walking away with my tail between my legs like I'd done so many times before, I said: "You know what, Gary? I'm sick of your (bleep)." He got up and got right in my face and said: "So what are you gonna do about it?" Quickly, I thought to myself, Jesus, I don't know what I'm going to do about it, but I can't back down now. I noticed that he had just brushed his teeth and there was a little toothpaste in the corner of his mouth, so I pointed to the corner of his mouth and said: "Hey, Gary, you've got a little toothpaste in the corner of your mouth." To this day, I have no idea why I said that. I just didn't have an answer to "So what are you gonna do about it?" I knew I couldn't say: "I'm gonna kick your butt" because I was a flabby 39-year-old sportswriter at the time and he was 26 and chiseled and no doubt would have beaten the hell out of me. His response to the toothpaste comment was this: He came at me with an overhand right that was intercepted by either Sam Perkins or David Wingate, thankfully, because it no doubt would have hurt my face. Then George Karl bear hugged me, to prevent me from charging Payton, figuring I had plans to do that, which I didn't. Then Payton and I just shouted "Bleep yous!" back and forth to each other before I was escorted out of the locker room. My punishment: I couldn't talk to Payton for 48 hours, which meant no Payton comments in my stories for two whole days. I didn't mind that at all. And I'm sure he didn't either. To his credit, from that day forward and through the NBA Finals, we had a decent professional relationship. But I always did better with him when other reporters asked questions and I listened in. Five or six years later when I covered the team again for one year, he was great. Now if I were to see him, I'd hope that it would be a case of no hard feelings. But I do know this: He wasn't my favorite player, and I sure as heck know that I wasn't his favorite reporter either. BACK TO THE ROAD TRIP: Once again, for the second consecutive day, nothing of interest happened. Willie my golden retriever ran around the Arco Arena parking lot chasing tennis balls and barked at other dogs outside the Lost Hills, Calif., Denny's, where I spent a couple of hours writing the Hawes column. Should make it to Peoria tomorrow unless I take a detour to Las Vegas, which is less than two hours from here.
